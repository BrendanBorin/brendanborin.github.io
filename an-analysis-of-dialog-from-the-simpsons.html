An Analysis of Dialog from The Simpsons

Date Sun 16 July 2017 Tags investing / trading / statistics / python / pandas

Kaggle has a number of datasets that one can download and play with. I recently found a dataset that contains detailed script data for all episodes of The Simpsons, one of my favorite cartoons. There are three files with information about the characters, the episodes, and all lines of dialog. In particular, the dialog file is quite detailed. Scenes are defined, and the speaker and timestamp are given for each spoken line. This is much more detailed than other datasets I have looked for in the past for other shows, some of which have all spoken words, but no speaker attached, making them much more difficult to analyze without watching all the episodes again and recording the data manually.

Questions to answer in this notebook:¶

In this post, I'd like to try to answer the following questions:

1) Who are the most important characters in term of number of lines/words?¶

2) Does the focus on characters change over time?¶

3) How many episodes do the different characters appear in?¶

4) Which characters tend to appear together?¶

5) Who has the greatest / most sophisticated vocabulary?¶

Create graph data linking characters¶

Here, I will calculate relationships between characters by counting the number of times they speak in a scene together. Only the fifty characters with the greatest number of lines will be used to cut down on the complexity of the graph below, which is created using a d3.js force graph.¶

I defined groups consisting of the Simpsons family, people from Springfield Elementary, people from the power plant, and everyone else. We will see if those groups tend to congregate in the graph.¶

In [4]:
import math
import itertools
from collections import defaultdict

#use only 50 characters to prevent overly complicated graph
short_list = list(lines.groupby('character_id').count().sort_values(by='normalized_text', ascending=False).index)
short_list = [int(i) for i in short_list][:50]

graph_data = defaultdict(lambda: 0)
characters = []

def process_line(x=None):
    global characters
    if x is None:
        characters = set(characters)
        for i, j in itertools.combinations(characters, 2):
            if i < j:
                graph_data[(i, j)] += 1
            else:
                graph_data[(j, i)] += 1
        return
    else:
        if x['speaking_line'] == False:
            characters = set(sorted(characters))
            for i, j in itertools.combinations(characters, 2):
                if i < j:
                    graph_data[(i, j)] += 1
                else:
                    graph_data[(j, i)] += 1
            characters = []
            return
        else:
            if not math.isnan(x.character_id) and int(x.character_id) in short_list:
                characters.append(int(x.character_id))        
        
lines.apply(lambda x: process_line(x), axis=1)

In the early range up to ~5000 words, there appears to be 3 groups. In the lowest group, we find some of the most important characters, including Homer, Marge, Bart, and Moe. I am not surprised that Homer has the shallowest vocab curve, but I am a bit surprised that Marge runs neck-and-neck with Homer most of the way. I guess I expected her to be significantly ahead of him. Another surprise to me was that Moe begins to overtake all main Simpsons family members except Lisa. Bart is also well ahead of Homer and Marge, but not Lisa, of course. Lisa falls in the middle group early on, but ends with a respectable vocabulary. Within the middle group, Mr. Burns and Principal Skinner are at the top, and at the 17,500 word mark, they easily have the greatest vocabularies. The high fliers early on include the Announcer, Kent Brockman, Comic Book Guy, Prof. Frink, and Mayor Joe Quimby. All except Kent Brockman have far fewer total words than other characters, but add new words at the greatest pace. Kent Brockman really stands out, adding new words at a faster pace all the way up to the 10,000 word mark. Within this group, Comic Book Guy surprised me the most!

I think this curve is a better way of measuring sophistication of speech because it lets you compare characters on an equal-word basis.
